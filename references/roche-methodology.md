# David Roche Coaching Methodology

Reference file for the running-coach skill. This codifies the core principles, session
designs, and athlete communication patterns drawn from David Roche's coaching philosophy
(Some Work, All Play / SWAP Running). Read this file when designing training plans,
structuring workouts, advising on race execution, or responding to athlete setbacks.

## Core Philosophy: Happy Runner, Fast Runner

Roche's foundational belief is that joy drives consistency, and consistency drives
performance. This isn't soft — it's strategic. An athlete who loves training runs more
miles over more years than one who white-knuckles through joyless suffering. Every coaching
decision should be filtered through: "Does this make the athlete want to come back tomorrow?"

Key implications:

- Training should feel like play more often than punishment
- Hard sessions should be satisfying, not soul-crushing
- If an athlete dreads a session type, redesign it — the adaptation isn't worth the
  motivation cost
- Recovery weeks are celebrated, not apologized for
- "The fastest ultrarunner is the happiest one"

## The Smooth Tempo Concept

Roche's signature workout philosophy. "Smooth" is the operative word — not "hard," not
"threshold," not "suffer."

### What Smooth Tempo Means

- **Comfortably hard**: breathing is rhythmic but you couldn't hold a full conversation
- **Sustainable**: the effort you could hold for 60+ minutes if you had to
- **Controlled**: at no point should it feel like you're fighting to hold on
- The athlete should finish a smooth tempo thinking "I could have gone harder" — that's
  the point. The restraint IS the training.

### How It Differs From Traditional Threshold Work

Traditional coaching: "Run at lactate threshold pace (X:XX/km) for Y minutes"

Roche approach: "Find the effort where everything clicks — your breathing, your cadence,
your posture — and hold it. The pace will take care of itself."

This distinction matters enormously for mountain running where pace is meaningless on
climbs. An athlete trained on effort-based tempo translates that skill directly to
sustained climbing. An athlete trained on pace-based tempo has to recalibrate for every
gradient.

### Application to Mountain Tempo

Mountain tempo is smooth tempo applied to climbs:

- Find a sustained climb (8-15% grade, 10-25 min duration)
- Run at smooth tempo effort — Z3+, RPE 7, rhythmic breathing
- The first few minutes should feel almost easy
- The last few minutes should feel like the end of a race climb
- If you're gasping, you went too hard. If you could chat, too easy.
- Progress by extending duration (12 -> 15 -> 18 -> 22 min), not intensity

## Effort Progression Within Sessions

Roche rarely programs even-split workouts. Instead, sessions have internal architecture
that teaches the athlete to find gears.

### Long Run Progression Pattern

Rather than "run 30k at Z2," a Roche-style long run builds:

1. **Easy opening** (first 30-40% of distance at Z2 — patient, no ego)
2. **Middle section** with structured climbing efforts (Z3 on climbs, Z2 recovery)
3. **Final section** at moderate-to-hard effort (Z3 on flats)
4. The athlete finishes feeling like they're running INTO the run, not surviving it

This teaches the athlete that tired doesn't mean slow — the exact skill needed at km 50+
of an ultra.

### Interval Progression Pattern

Rather than "6 x 1km all at the same pace," Roche often uses:

- Progressive rep lengths across mesocycles (1km -> 1.2km -> 1.5km)
- Cut-down rest (same pace, shrinking recovery) to simulate race-day lactate accumulation
- Combination sessions (800m reps + 200m "pops") that hit multiple energy systems

The variety keeps the athlete mentally engaged and teaches pace flexibility.

## Strides as Free Speed

One of Roche's most distinctive coaching signatures. Strides appear in almost every
non-rest day across the entire training plan.

### The Principle

- 20-second accelerations at ~mile effort (Z4-5)
- Full recovery between (60s walk/jog)
- Zero fatigue cost when done correctly
- Maintain neuromuscular coordination and fast-twitch recruitment year-round
- They're "free" — they add speed stimulus without adding training load

### How to Program Them

- 4-8 strides at the end of easy runs
- Mix flat strides (speed) and hill strides (power) based on training phase
- Continue through taper — strides replace structured intervals in the final 2 weeks
- The athlete should feel faster AFTER strides than before — if not, they're going too
  hard or too long

### Hill Strides Specifically

- 20 seconds on 8-12% grade at near-max controlled effort
- Drive off the balls of the feet, pump arms, stay tall
- These are strength work disguised as speed work
- Build posterior chain power (glutes, hamstrings) that fuels climbing
- Progress from 6 -> 8 per session as the plan advances

## Back-to-Back Weekend Philosophy

Central to Roche's ultra preparation methodology.

### Why It Works

The Saturday long run creates fatigue. The Sunday moderate run teaches the body to
function within that fatigue. This combination:

- Simulates late-race conditions (running on damaged muscle fibers)
- Forces recruitment of deep muscle fibers that only activate when primary fibers are
  exhausted
- Builds mental resilience for the "dark patch" in ultra racing
- Creates a larger cumulative training stimulus than either session alone

### How to Execute

- **Saturday**: the primary stimulus (long run with vert, structured efforts, fueling practice)
- **Sunday**: honest effort on tired legs (Z2 base with Z3 surges on climbs)
- Sunday should feel terrible for the first 20-30 minutes, then gradually unlock
- That "unlocking" IS the adaptation — don't cut the run short before it happens
- Include strides at the end of Sunday if legs cooperate — signals to the body that speed
  exists even when fatigued

### Progressive Overload

The weekend combination grows across the plan:

| Phase | Saturday | Sunday | Total |
|-------|----------|--------|-------|
| Early | 18k | 12k | 30k |
| Mid | 28k | 16k | 44k |
| Peak | 36k | 18k | 54k |

By peak weekends, the athlete is exceeding B-race distance in training.

## How Roche Handles Setbacks

This is where coaching philosophy matters most. Roche's approach to illness, injury,
missed training, and bad races:

### Reframe, Don't Recalculate

When an athlete misses training:

- Never say "you've lost X weeks of fitness" — that's demoralizing and usually inaccurate
- Instead: "Here's what you haven't lost, here's what needs rebuilding, here's the path"
- Quantify the actual physiological impact (detraining timelines, which systems are
  resilient vs fragile)
- Contextualize within the plan — missing speed work during a mountain phase matters less
  than the athlete thinks

### Never Chase Missed Volume

- Missed training is gone. Attempting to make it up creates injury risk.
- The plan moves forward from where the athlete IS, not where they were supposed to be
- Scale the current week's sessions to current fitness, not planned fitness
- Trust that the body absorbs training non-linearly — 80% of the plan executed well beats
  100% of the plan executed while injured

### Protect the A-Race From Everything

- B-race goals are expendable. A-race preparation is not.
- If a secondary goal (time trial PR, B-race placement) conflicts with A-race readiness,
  the secondary goal gets parked without drama
- Frame this as strategic, not defeatist: "We're banking that fitness for July, not
  spending it in April"

### Separate Process From Outcome

After a bad workout or race:

- Evaluate the PROCESS (pacing, nutrition, effort management) separately from the RESULT
- A perfectly executed race that produces a mediocre time is a success
- A PR achieved through reckless pacing that leads to injury is a failure
- Train the athlete to judge themselves on execution, not the clock

## Race Execution Philosophy

### Conservative Start, Strong Finish

- First 25% of any race should feel "too easy" — that's correct pacing
- Middle 50% should feel like smooth tempo — controlled, rhythmic, sustainable
- Final 25% is where fitness and discipline pay off — the athlete who held back early
  now has reserves to push
- Negative-split the EFFORT (not necessarily the pace — terrain makes pace meaningless)

### Climbs Are Won by Patience

- Never race anyone uphill in the first half
- Power-hike steep sections (>20% grade) without ego — it's faster per energy unit
- Find the climbing rhythm where breathing is controlled and cadence is consistent
- Eat on climbs — don't wait for flats (at race intensity, GI stress spikes on climbs,
  so this must be practiced in training)

### Descents Are Won by Technique

- Short stride, high cadence (180+ spm), forward lean from ankles
- Eyes 3-5 meters ahead, not at your feet
- Midfoot landing — never brake with heels (destroys quads)
- Arms relaxed and slightly wide for balance
- "Flow" mindset — work with gravity, not against it
- Races are won or lost on descents because most athletes brake and destroy their legs
  for the remaining distance

### Fueling Is the Fourth Discipline

- Time-based intake every 20 minutes, not distance-based
- Never skip a feed window, even if not hungry — by the time you're hungry, it's too late
- If nauseous, reduce to half portion but keep eating
- Practice the exact protocol in training until it's automatic
- Progressive gut training across the plan: 80g/hr -> 90 -> 100 -> 110 -> 120g/hr

## Communication Patterns

How Roche-style coaching sounds in practice:

### Explaining a Workout

Not: "Do 5 x 1km at 3:48/km with 90s recovery"

But: "5 x 1km at 3:48-3:52. Hard but controlled — you should finish each rep feeling like
you could do one more. Full recovery between. We're building the aerobic engine here, not
testing it. If rep 5 feels significantly harder than rep 1, you started too fast."

### Responding to a Bad Session

Not: "Don't worry about it, you'll bounce back"

But: "4:14 pace feeling unsustainable at rep 4 tells us your lungs are rate-limiting, not
your legs. Post-infection, respiratory capacity is the last thing to recover. Your muscles
and cardiovascular system are ahead of where your breathing is. When the lungs catch up —
typically 1-2 weeks after you feel 100% — the speed will still be there."

### Motivating Through Understanding

Not: "You've got this! Stay positive!"

But: "If 3x20 min mountain tempo at Z3+ feels manageable, you can pace every climb at
Davos. That's not motivation — that's math. The training has prepared you for exactly
this duration at exactly this effort."

### Defending Rest

Not: "Take it easy if you need to"

But: "This is where adaptation happens. The hard sessions created the stimulus. The
recovery week is where your body actually builds the fitness. Skipping rest is like
putting seeds in the ground and then digging them up to check if they're growing."
